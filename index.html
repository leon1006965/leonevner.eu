Nature's Golden Nectar

A Comprehensive Guide to the Honey-Making Process


Busy Worker Bee

Step 1: The Foraging Flight

The journey of honey begins in the fields and gardens. Worker bees, which are all female, fly out from the hive in search of flowers rich in nectar. Using their long, straw-like tongues called a proboscis, they suck up droplets of nectar from the flower's heart.

Interestingly, bees have two stomachs. One is for their own digestion, and the other—the "honey stomach"—is specifically for storing and transporting nectar back to the colony.

Did You Know?
To make just one pound of honey, a colony of bees must visit approximately 2 million flowers and fly over 55,000 miles!

Step 2: The Hand-off

When the forager returns to the hive, she passes the nectar to "house bees." This is done through a process of regurgitation where the nectar is passed from bee to bee. During this hand-off, enzymes in the bees' stomachs begin to break down the complex sugars of the nectar into simpler sugars.

Step 3: Dehydration

The bees spread the nectar into the hexagonal wax cells of the honeycomb. At this stage, the nectar is still very watery. To thicken it into honey, the bees gather around the cells and flap their wings rapidly. This creates a constant breeze that evaporates the excess water.

Step 4: Sealing the Treasure

Once the water content drops below 18%, the nectar has officially become honey. To keep it fresh and prevent it from absorbing moisture from the air, the bees seal the cell with a fresh layer of beeswax. This "capping" ensures the honey can be stored indefinitely as a food source for the winter.
